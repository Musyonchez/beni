How to write every section of the report, with tone, length, and content rules
================================================================================

This file is the companion to project_steps.txt.
Steps tells you WHAT to do. This file tells you HOW to write it.
Follow this so the report reads consistently from Chapter 1 to Chapter 7,
even if written across multiple weeks or by different contributors.


================================================================================
GENERAL WRITING RULES (apply everywhere)
================================================================================

TONE
  - Academic and formal. No slang, no contractions (don't → do not).
  - Write in third person: "The system allows farmers to..." not "our system..."
  - Be specific. Never write vague sentences like "the system is very good."
    Instead: "FarmLink reduces post-harvest losses by connecting farmers
    directly to buyers within their locality."

TENSE
  - Background and literature review: past tense ("Studies have shown...")
  - System description and objectives: present/future tense ("The system will...")
  - Findings and results: past tense ("Respondents indicated...")

LENGTH TARGETS
  - Minimum total: 6,000 words (excluding diagrams, tables, appendices)
  - Chapter 1: ~1,000–1,200 words
  - Chapter 2: ~1,500–2,000 words
  - Chapter 3: ~600–800 words
  - Chapter 4: ~800–1,000 words (text only, not counting diagrams)
  - Chapter 5: ~600–800 words
  - Chapter 6: ~500–700 words
  - Chapter 7: ~400–600 words (text only, not counting screenshots)

CITATIONS
  - APA 7th edition throughout
  - Every claim about another country or system needs a citation
  - Format: (Author, Year) in-text, full reference in Section 8.0
  - Example: (Food and Agriculture Organization, 2021)
  - If no author, use the organisation name: (Ministry of Agriculture Kenya, 2020)
  - Do not cite Wikipedia. Use FAO, government sites, journals, conference papers.

HEADINGS
  - Use the exact numbering from the documentation format (1.0, 1.1, 1.2...)
  - Do not skip numbering or rename sections

PARAGRAPHS
  - Minimum 3 sentences per paragraph
  - Each paragraph = one idea. New idea = new paragraph.
  - No bullet points in the body of chapters (only in requirement lists in Ch.4)

CHAPTER ONE – INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

--- 1.0 INTRODUCTION (3–4 sentences) ---

Purpose: Orient the reader. What is the project and why does it exist?

Write:
  Sentence 1 – What the system is.
  Sentence 2 – The context/problem that motivated it.
  Sentence 3 – What the system does to address the problem.
  Sentence 4 – Overview of what this chapter covers.

Example structure (fill in with real content):
  "FarmLink is a mobile-based agricultural produce eMarketplace designed to
  connect Kenyan smallholder farmers directly with buyers, eliminating the
  need for intermediaries. The majority of Kenyan farmers operate at a
  subsistence level partly due to lack of access to fair and direct markets,
  leading to low income and significant post-harvest losses. FarmLink addresses
  this by providing a platform where farmers can list produce, buyers can
  discover and purchase directly, and transactions are completed securely via
  M-Pesa. This chapter presents the background, problem statement, objectives,
  scope, limitations, and significance of the project."


--- 1.1 BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY (1.5–2 pages) ---

Purpose: Show the system exists elsewhere and is needed here.

Structure – write 5–7 lines for each:

  THREE DEVELOPED COUNTRIES
    USA     – FarmersWeb: B2B platform connecting local farms to restaurants
              and institutions. Launched 2013, operates across major US cities.
    UK      – Farmdrop: Online farm-to-consumer grocery platform connecting
              local British farmers to urban buyers. (Cite: farmdrop.com)
    Australia – Farmhub or AgriDigital: digital grain marketplace and supply
              chain platform. Widely adopted by Australian grain farmers.

  TWO AFRICAN COUNTRIES
    Nigeria – Farmcrowdy / TradeDepot: agri-tech platforms connecting farmers
              to markets and investors. Significant adoption since 2016.
    Kenya   – Twiga Foods: B2B platform sourcing produce from farmers and
              distributing to urban vendors via a logistics network.
              (Note: this is a competitor — compare and contrast with FarmLink)

  KENYA CONTEXT
    - About 75% of Kenya's population depends on agriculture (FAO, 2020)
    - Smallholder farmers (< 2 acres) produce 75% of total farm output
    - Most sell through brokers/middlemen at reduced prices
    - Post-harvest losses estimated at 30–40% of produce (MoA Kenya, 2019)
    - Mobile internet penetration in Kenya reached 43% in 2023 (CA Kenya, 2023)
      — creating a viable environment for a mobile eMarketplace

IMPORTANT: Do not copy-paste from websites. Read, understand, rewrite in
your own words, then cite the source.


--- 1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT (half page, ~250 words) ---

Purpose: Convince the reader the problem is real and the system is necessary.

The 4 problems for FarmLink:
  1. Overdependence on middlemen – farmers receive as little as 20–30% of
     the final retail price, reducing their income and discouraging production.
  2. No direct digital channel – farmers lack a platform to reach buyers
     outside their immediate geography, limiting market access.
  3. Buyers cannot verify produce quality or origin before purchase, leading
     to mistrust and preference for supermarkets over local farmers.
  4. Post-harvest losses – without a quick way to find buyers, perishable
     produce rots before it can be sold, costing farmers significant income.


--- 1.3 PROJECT OBJECTIVES ---

  1.3.1 OVERALL GOAL (1 sentence)
    "To design and develop FarmLink, an online agricultural produce
    eMarketplace for Kenyan smallholder farmers."

  1.3.2 SYSTEMS DESIGN AND DEVELOPMENT OBJECTIVES
    Write each as: "To implement [feature] in order to [benefit]."

    i.   To implement a geolocation module that enables buyers to discover
         farmers and available produce within their geographical proximity.
    ii.  To develop a shopping cart and M-Pesa payment integration that
         allows buyers to purchase produce securely within the application.
    iii. To build a real-time order tracking feature that allows buyers to
         monitor the status of their orders from farm to delivery.
    iv.  To create a seller rating and review system that allows buyers to
         rate farmers and produce quality, promoting accountability and trust.

  1.3.3 SPECIFIC RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
    1.3.3.1 To identify the traditional challenges faced by smallholder
            farmers when using manual and intermediary-based market systems
            in Kenya.
    1.3.3.2 To determine the benefits of adopting an online agricultural
            produce eMarketplace for Kenyan smallholder farmers.


--- 1.4 PROJECT QUESTIONS ---

  1. What are the traditional challenges faced by Kenyan smallholder farmers
     when using manual and intermediary-based market systems?
  2. What are the benefits of adopting an online agricultural produce
     eMarketplace for Kenyan smallholder farmers?


--- 1.5 SCOPE OF THE PROJECT (~200–300 words) ---

  USER ROLES AND FUNCTIONALITY
    Farmer (Seller):
      Register/login, list produce (name, price, quantity, photos, location),
      manage listings, view and confirm orders, view ratings and earnings

    Buyer (Consumer):
      Register/login, browse produce by category or location, view farmer
      profiles, add to cart, pay via M-Pesa, track orders, leave reviews

    Admin:
      Manage all users, moderate listings, view platform-wide reports
      (sales volume, active farmers, popular categories)

  STAKEHOLDERS
    Smallholder farmers, Buyers/consumers, System administrator,
    Safaricom (M-Pesa gateway), Developer (Beni Ishimwe)

  IMPLEMENTATION TARGET
    "The system will be prototyped and tested targeting smallholder farmers
    in the peri-urban areas of Nairobi County (Limuru, Kikuyu, Kiambu),
    with buyers primarily in Nairobi City."

  OUT OF SCOPE
    - Logistics and physical delivery are not managed by the system
    - The system will not handle insurance or government subsidy processing
    - Bulk/wholesale B2B transactions (restaurants, supermarkets) are not
      included in this version


--- 1.6 LIMITATIONS OF THE STUDY (~150–200 words) ---

  1. Time – 14 weeks limits development to a prototype.
     Solution: Focus on core features; document future work in Chapter 6.
  2. Internet access – some target farmers in peri-urban areas have
     limited or expensive data.
     Solution: App designed to be lightweight; key screens cached offline.
  3. M-Pesa sandbox – full payment testing requires a Safaricom business
     account; only sandbox/test mode available as a student.
     Solution: Use Daraja API sandbox and document live deployment steps.
  4. Sample size – access to farmers for the questionnaire may be limited.
     Solution: Use purposive sampling; target farmer cooperatives or SACCOs.


--- 1.7 SIGNIFICANCE OF THE STUDY (~200 words) ---

  Paragraph 1 – SDG 2:
    "This project contributes to Sustainable Development Goal 2 (Zero Hunger),
    which calls for ending hunger, achieving food security and improved
    nutrition, and promoting sustainable agriculture. By providing farmers
    with direct market access, FarmLink supports target 2.3, which aims to
    double the agricultural productivity and income of small-scale food
    producers by 2030..."

  Paragraph 2 – SDG 8:
    "FarmLink also contributes to SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth)
    by enabling smallholder farmers to earn a fair and direct income from
    their produce, reducing economic exploitation by intermediaries..."

  Paragraph 3 – AU Agenda 2063:
    "The project aligns with the African Union's Agenda 2063 Aspiration 1,
    which envisions a prosperous Africa built on inclusive growth and
    sustainable development. Digital agricultural marketplaces are identified
    as a key enabler of rural economic transformation across the continent..."

  Paragraph 4 – Local impact:
    "Within Kenya, FarmLink has the potential to increase farmer income,
    reduce post-harvest losses, and improve food availability in urban areas
    by shortening the supply chain between farm and consumer."

CHAPTER TWO – LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

--- 2.0 INTRODUCTION (3–4 sentences) ---

  "This chapter reviews existing online agricultural eMarketplace systems
  from around the world, analysing their features, strengths, and weaknesses.
  It further examines literature related to the two specific research
  objectives: challenges of traditional market systems and benefits of
  digital agricultural eMarketplaces."


--- 2.1 EXISTING SYSTEMS ---

  System 1 – FarmersWeb (USA)
    URL: farmersweb.com
    B2B platform connecting local farms to chefs, restaurants, and grocery
    buyers. Features: product catalogue, order management, delivery scheduling.
    Strength: Strong B2B focus, clean UI, trusted by over 600 farms.
    Weakness: USA only, B2B only (no consumer access), subscription fee required.

  System 2 – Farmdrop (UK)
    Farm-to-consumer online grocery platform. Buyers order from local British
    farms; Farmdrop handles logistics.
    Strength: Consumer-facing, strong sustainability branding, quality control.
    Weakness: Discontinued in 2022 due to logistics costs — important lesson
    on keeping delivery out of scope for v1.

  System 3 – Twiga Foods (Kenya)
    B2B agri-logistics platform. Sources from smallholder farmers and delivers
    to urban kiosks and vendors via a cold chain.
    Strength: Kenya-specific, large farmer network, mobile-first.
    Weakness: B2B only, farmers cannot set their own prices, platform takes margin.

  2.1.4 COMPARISON TABLE
    Columns: Feature | FarmersWeb (USA) | Farmdrop (UK) | Twiga Foods (Kenya)
    Rows: Cost, Easy to Install, Easy to Use, Efficient Database,
          Good Security, Vulnerability Assessment


--- 2.2 LITERATURE REVIEW ON RESEARCH OBJECTIVES ---

  2.2.1 CHALLENGES OF TRADITIONAL/MANUAL MARKET SYSTEMS (8 challenges)

    1. Exploitation by middlemen – farmers receive a fraction of retail price
    2. Limited geographic market reach – farmers can only sell locally
    3. Price opacity – farmers have no visibility into market prices before selling
    4. Post-harvest losses – no fast channel to find buyers for perishables
    5. Lack of produce traceability – buyers cannot verify origin or quality
    6. High transport costs – farmers travel to markets without guaranteed sales
    7. No credit or financial record – informal sales leave no transaction history
    8. Seasonal income instability – no forward orders or pre-booking system

  2.2.2 BENEFITS OF AN ONLINE AGRICULTURAL EMARKETPLACE (8 benefits)

    1. Direct farmer-to-buyer connection removes intermediary exploitation
    2. Expanded market reach beyond local geography via mobile internet
    3. Price transparency – farmers can set and compare prices
    4. Reduced post-harvest losses through faster buyer discovery
    5. Digital transaction records support financial inclusion and credit access
    6. Geolocation reduces buyer search time and delivery distance
    7. Ratings and reviews build trust and incentivise quality produce
    8. Data insights for farmers (which produce sells fastest, peak demand times)


--- 2.3 CONCLUSION ---

  "The literature demonstrates that traditional market systems consistently
  disadvantage smallholder farmers through price exploitation and limited
  market access. While digital solutions exist in developed countries and
  emerging markets like Kenya, none are tailored to the direct consumer-farmer
  mobile experience that FarmLink proposes. This gap justifies the development
  of an affordable, mobile-first eMarketplace solution designed for the
  Kenyan context."

CHAPTER THREE – METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

  Research Design: DESCRIPTIVE
    "A descriptive research design was adopted to describe the current state
    of market access for smallholder farmers and the needs of buyers, without
    manipulating any variables."

  Population and Sampling:
    Target: Smallholder farmers in peri-urban Nairobi + urban buyers
    Sample: 30–50 respondents
    Method: Purposive sampling (they are direct users of the proposed system)
    Where to find respondents: farmer cooperatives, local markets (Kangemi,
    Wakulima Market), SACCOs, neighbours/community members

  Data Collection:
    Primary: Questionnaire (Appendix 9.1)
    Secondary: FAO reports, Ministry of Agriculture Kenya, journals

  Data Analysis:
    Percentages and bar charts from questionnaire responses
    "X% of farmer respondents indicated that..."

  Testing Plan:
    Unit testing – each module (auth, listings, cart, payment, tracking)
    Integration testing – full flow: list → discover → buy → track
    UAT – real farmers and buyers test the prototype

  Ethical Considerations:
    - Informed consent from all respondents
    - Anonymous questionnaires (no names collected)
    - Data used for academic purposes only

CHAPTER FOUR – SYSTEMS ANALYSIS AND DESIGN
================================================================================

--- FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS ---

  FR1  – Farmers shall be able to register, log in, and manage their profile
  FR2  – Farmers shall be able to list, edit, and remove produce listings
  FR3  – Buyers shall be able to browse and search produce by category and location
  FR4  – The system shall display nearby farmers based on buyer's geolocation
  FR5  – Buyers shall be able to add items to a cart and checkout via M-Pesa
  FR6  – The system shall update order status in real time (placed → delivered)
  FR7  – Buyers shall be able to leave ratings and reviews for farmers
  FR8  – Admins shall be able to manage users, listings, and view reports

--- NON-FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS ---

  NFR1 – Security: Passwords hashed (bcrypt), JWT for session tokens
  NFR2 – Usability: A first-time user can complete a purchase within 5 minutes
  NFR3 – Performance: API responses under 2 seconds on a 3G connection
  NFR4 – Availability: App accessible 24/7 for browsing and ordering
  NFR5 – Compatibility: Works on Android 8+ and iOS 13+

  NOTE ON STACK CHOICES (for Section 7.2 justifications):
    React Native – cross-platform (Android + iOS) from one codebase; large
                   community; Expo simplifies testing on physical devices
    Node.js      – fast, non-blocking I/O ideal for real-time order updates;
                   JavaScript across frontend and backend reduces context switching
    MongoDB      – flexible document schema suits variable produce listings
                   (different fields per category); scales horizontally

--- DIAGRAMS – WHAT EACH ONE MUST SHOW ---

  USE CASE DIAGRAM
    Actors: Farmer, Buyer, Admin, M-Pesa Gateway
    Farmer: Register, Login, List Produce, Manage Listings, View Orders,
            View Earnings, View Reviews
    Buyer:  Register, Login, Browse Produce, Search by Location, Add to Cart,
            Pay via M-Pesa, Track Order, Leave Review
    Admin:  Manage Users, Moderate Listings, View Reports

  FLOWCHART (buyer purchase flow)
    Start → Open App → Register/Login → Browse/Search Produce →
    View Product → Add to Cart → Checkout → Enter M-Pesa PIN →
    Payment Confirmed → Order Placed → Farmer Notified →
    Order Dispatched → Buyer Tracks → Delivered → Leave Review

  CLASS DIAGRAM
    Classes: User, Farmer, Buyer, Admin, Product, Category, Order,
             OrderItem, Payment, Review, Notification
    Farmer extends User; Buyer extends User; Admin extends User
    Farmer has many Products; Buyer places many Orders;
    Order has many OrderItems; Order has one Payment; Order has one Review

  DATA FLOW DIAGRAM
    Level 0: FarmLink System ↔ Farmer, Buyer, Admin, M-Pesa Gateway
    Level 1: User Management, Product Management, Order Management,
             Payment Processing, Notification Service, Reporting
    Level 2: Break down Order Management into:
             Browse, Add to Cart, Checkout, Track, Complete, Review

  ERD – KEY COLLECTIONS (MongoDB documents)
    users        { _id, name, email, password_hash, role, phone, created_at }
    farmers      { _id, user_id, location, bio, rating, total_sales }
    products     { _id, farmer_id, category_id, name, price, quantity,
                   unit, images[], location, available, created_at }
    categories   { _id, name }
    orders       { _id, buyer_id, farmer_id, items[], total, status,
                   payment_ref, created_at, updated_at }
    reviews      { _id, order_id, buyer_id, farmer_id, rating, comment, date }
    notifications{ _id, user_id, message, type, read, created_at }

  SEQUENCE DIAGRAMS
    Farmer: Login → List Product → Receive Order Notification → Confirm Order
    Buyer:  Login → Search Produce → Add to Cart → Pay (M-Pesa) →
            Receive Confirmation → Track Order → Leave Review
    Admin:  Login → View Reports → Manage Users → Moderate Listings
