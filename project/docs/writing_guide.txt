================================================================================
MEDBOOK – DETAILED WRITING GUIDE
How to write every section of the report, with tone, length, and content rules
================================================================================

This file is the companion to project_steps.txt.
Steps tells you WHAT to do. This file tells you HOW to write it.
Follow this so the report reads consistently from Chapter 1 to Chapter 7,
even if written across multiple weeks or by different contributors.


================================================================================
GENERAL WRITING RULES (apply everywhere)
================================================================================

TONE
  - Academic and formal. No slang, no contractions (don't → do not).
  - Write in third person: "The system allows patients to..." not "our system..."
  - Be specific. Never write vague sentences like "the system is very good."
    Instead: "The system reduces patient waiting time by enabling online booking."

TENSE
  - Background and literature review: past tense ("Studies have shown...")
  - System description and objectives: present/future tense ("The system will...")
  - Findings and results: past tense ("Respondents indicated...")

LENGTH TARGETS
  - Minimum total: 6,000 words (excluding diagrams, tables, appendices)
  - Chapter 1: ~1,000–1,200 words
  - Chapter 2: ~1,500–2,000 words
  - Chapter 3: ~600–800 words
  - Chapter 4: ~800–1,000 words (text only, not counting diagrams)
  - Chapter 5: ~600–800 words
  - Chapter 6: ~500–700 words
  - Chapter 7: ~400–600 words (text only, not counting screenshots)

CITATIONS
  - APA 7th edition throughout
  - Every claim about another country or system needs a citation
  - Format: (Author, Year) in-text, full reference in Section 8.0
  - Example: (World Health Organization, 2021)
  - If no author, use the organisation name: (Ministry of Health Kenya, 2020)
  - Do not cite Wikipedia. Use WHO, government sites, journals, conference papers.

HEADINGS
  - Use the exact numbering from the documentation format (1.0, 1.1, 1.2...)
  - Do not skip numbering or rename sections

PARAGRAPHS
  - Minimum 3 sentences per paragraph
  - Each paragraph = one idea. New idea = new paragraph.
  - No bullet points in the body of chapters (only in requirement lists in Ch.4)


================================================================================
CHAPTER ONE – INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

--- 1.0 INTRODUCTION (3–4 sentences) ---

Purpose: Orient the reader. What is the project and why does it exist?

Write:
  Sentence 1 – What the system is.
  Sentence 2 – The context/problem that motivated it.
  Sentence 3 – What the system does to address the problem.
  Sentence 4 – Overview of what this chapter covers.

Example structure (fill in with real content):
  "MedBook is a web-based health appointment booking and management system
  designed to improve access to healthcare services in Kenya. The increasing
  demand for healthcare, combined with inefficient manual booking processes,
  has resulted in long queues and poor patient experiences in many Kenyan
  clinics. MedBook enables patients to search for doctors, book appointments
  online, and receive automated reminders, while allowing healthcare
  administrators to efficiently manage schedules and patient records.
  This chapter presents the background, problem statement, objectives, scope,
  limitations, and significance of the project."


--- 1.1 BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY (1.5–2 pages) ---

Purpose: Show the system exists elsewhere and is needed here.

Structure – write 5–7 lines for each:

  THREE DEVELOPED COUNTRIES (pick from: UK, USA, Australia, Canada, Germany)
    What system/platform exists there, when it was introduced, what it does,
    and what impact it has had. Cite each.

    Suggested systems to research:
      USA     – Zocdoc (zocdoc.com) – online doctor search and booking
      UK      – NHS App / Patient Access – national health booking platform
      Germany – Doctolib – appointment booking system widely used in Europe

  TWO AFRICAN COUNTRIES (pick from: South Africa, Rwanda, Nigeria, Ghana)
    Same format as above. Show Africa is also adopting this.

    Suggested:
      South Africa – Quro Medical / HealthQ
      Rwanda       – Babylon Health partnership with government (2019)

  KENYA CONTEXT
    Describe the current situation in Kenya:
    - Most clinics use paper registers or phone calls to book appointments
    - Urban hospitals like KNH and Aga Khan have partial digital systems
    - Rural and mid-sized clinics have no digital booking at all
    - Patients sometimes travel to find a doctor is unavailable
    Cite: Ministry of Health Kenya reports or Kenya Health Policy documents.

IMPORTANT: Do not copy-paste from websites. Read, understand, rewrite in
your own words, then cite the source.


--- 1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT (half page, ~250 words) ---

Purpose: Convince the reader the problem is real and the system is necessary.

Rules:
  - State exactly 4 problems (numbered)
  - Each problem: 2–3 sentences. State the problem, then explain its impact.
  - Problems must be specific to the Kenyan/manual booking context.
  - Do NOT say "the system is slow" — say what the consequence of that slowness is.

The 4 problems for MedBook:
  1. Long physical queues – patients spend hours waiting, reducing time for work/school
  2. No-show appointments – no reminder system means wasted doctor slots and lost revenue
  3. No visibility of doctor availability – patients travel to clinics only to be turned away
  4. Poor record keeping – paper-based files are easily lost, making continuity of care difficult


--- 1.3 PROJECT OBJECTIVES ---

  1.3.1 OVERALL GOAL (1 sentence)
    "To design and develop MedBook, an online health appointment booking and
    management system for healthcare facilities in Nairobi, Kenya."

  1.3.2 SYSTEMS DESIGN AND DEVELOPMENT OBJECTIVES
    These are the 4 technical features YOU are building. Write each as:
    "To implement [feature] in order to [benefit]."

    i.   To implement a doctor search and filtering module that allows patients
         to find doctors by specialty, name, and location.
    ii.  To develop an automated SMS and email reminder system that notifies
         patients of upcoming appointments to reduce no-shows.
    iii. To build a patient dashboard that displays appointment history,
         upcoming bookings, and basic medical notes.
    iv.  To create a real-time appointment slot management interface for
         healthcare administrators to manage doctor availability.

  1.3.3 SPECIFIC RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
    These are the research questions — NOT the system features.

    1.3.3.1 To identify the traditional challenges associated with manual
            health appointment booking systems in Kenyan healthcare facilities.
    1.3.3.2 To determine the benefits of adopting an online health appointment
            booking and management system in Kenyan healthcare facilities.


--- 1.4 PROJECT QUESTIONS (2 questions matching 1.3.3) ---

  1. What are the traditional challenges associated with manual health
     appointment booking systems in Kenyan healthcare facilities?
  2. What are the benefits of adopting an online health appointment booking
     and management system in Kenyan healthcare facilities?


--- 1.5 SCOPE OF THE PROJECT (~200–300 words) ---

Purpose: Define boundaries. What the system does, who uses it, and where.

Cover:
  SYSTEM FUNCTIONALITY – summarize the 3 user roles and what each can do
    Patient: register, search doctors, book/cancel appointments, get reminders
    Doctor/Staff: manage availability, view bookings, update appointment status
    Admin: manage all users, view system-wide reports

  STAKEHOLDERS
    Patients, Doctors, Clinic Administrators, System Developer (you)

  LOCATION / IMPLEMENTATION TARGET
    "The system will be prototyped and tested at a healthcare facility in
    Nairobi, Kenya, targeting small to medium-sized clinics with 2–20 doctors."

  WHAT IS OUT OF SCOPE (important – sets expectations)
    - The system will not process insurance claims
    - The system will not integrate with national health databases (e.g. NHIF)
    - Telemedicine / video consultations are not included in this version


--- 1.6 LIMITATIONS OF THE STUDY (~150–200 words) ---

Purpose: Be honest about constraints. Shows academic maturity.

Format: State limitation → then immediately state how you will manage it.

Suggested limitations:
  1. Time – 14 weeks is short for a full production system.
     Solution: Focus on a working prototype with core features.
  2. Sample size – questionnaire may reach a small number of respondents.
     Solution: Use purposive sampling targeting clinic staff and patients.
  3. Internet access – some target users may have limited internet.
     Solution: System designed to be lightweight and mobile-friendly.
  4. Budget – no funds for paid hosting or SMS API beyond free tier.
     Solution: Use Twilio free tier for SMS and XAMPP for local hosting.


--- 1.7 SIGNIFICANCE OF THE STUDY (~200 words) ---

Purpose: Justify why this project matters beyond your grade.

Paragraph 1 – SDG link:
  "This project is directly relevant to Sustainable Development Goal 3
  (Good Health and Well-being), which calls for ensuring healthy lives and
  promoting well-being for all at all ages. Specifically, MedBook supports
  target 3.8, which aims to achieve universal health coverage including
  access to quality essential healthcare services..."

Paragraph 2 – AU Agenda 2063 link:
  "MedBook also contributes to the African Union's Agenda 2063 Aspiration 1,
  which envisions a prosperous Africa built on inclusive growth. Digital health
  systems are a key driver of this aspiration, enabling African citizens to
  access services without the barriers of geography or physical queues..."

Paragraph 3 – Local impact:
  "Within Kenya, the system has the potential to reduce patient congestion
  in urban clinics, improve doctor utilisation rates, and create a digital
  health record trail that supports continuity of care..."

CHAPTER TWO – LITERATURE REVIEW
================================================================================

--- 2.0 INTRODUCTION (3–4 sentences) ---

  Briefly state what this chapter covers:
  "This chapter reviews existing online health appointment systems from around
  the world, comparing their features, strengths, and weaknesses. It also
  examines literature related to the two specific research objectives:
  challenges of manual booking systems and benefits of digital alternatives."


--- 2.1 EXISTING SYSTEMS (each: 5–7 lines + cite) ---

  Format for each system:
    Name and country → what it does → who uses it → key features →
    strengths → weaknesses → cite

  System 1 – Zocdoc (USA)
    URL: zocdoc.com
    What it does: Allows patients to search for doctors by specialty,
    insurance, and location, and book same-day appointments online.
    Strength: Large doctor network, real-time availability, insurance filtering.
    Weakness: Only available in the USA, requires insurance details, costly for clinics.

  System 2 – Doctolib (France/Germany/Italy)
    URL: doctolib.fr
    What it does: A European appointment platform used by over 300,000
    healthcare professionals. Includes teleconsultation features.
    Strength: Multi-language, widely adopted, integrated with hospital systems.
    Weakness: Not localised for African markets, expensive subscription model.

  System 3 – HealthQ (South Africa)
    What it does: A South African digital health platform offering appointment
    booking, queue management, and patient check-in.
    Strength: Africa-focused, queue management feature, accessible via mobile.
    Weakness: Limited to South Africa, not open source, no offline mode.

  2.1.4 COMPARISON TABLE
    Columns: Feature | Zocdoc (USA) | Doctolib (France) | HealthQ (S.Africa)
    Rows: Cost, Easy to Install, Easy to Use, Efficient Database,
          Good Security, Vulnerability Assessment
    Use: Tick (✓) = Yes, X = No, or "Partial"


--- 2.2 LITERATURE REVIEW ON RESEARCH OBJECTIVES ---

  2.2.1 CHALLENGES OF MANUAL/OLD SYSTEMS (state and explain 8)

  Write each as: bold heading → 2–3 sentence explanation → cite if possible.

  Suggested 8 challenges:
    1. Long waiting times and patient congestion
    2. High rate of missed/no-show appointments due to no reminders
    3. Difficulty retrieving patient records from paper files
    4. Scheduling conflicts and double-bookings by administrative staff
    5. Limited operating hours – patients can only book during clinic hours
    6. No data for decision making (no appointment statistics or reports)
    7. High administrative workload on receptionists
    8. Lack of transparency – patients do not know doctor availability in advance

  2.2.2 BENEFITS OF THE NEW SYSTEM (state and explain 8)

  Suggested 8 benefits:
    1. 24/7 appointment booking access for patients
    2. Automated reminders reduce no-shows
    3. Reduced queues and waiting time at clinics
    4. Centralised digital patient records for easy retrieval
    5. Real-time visibility of doctor availability
    6. Reduced administrative workload
    7. Data-driven reports to support clinic management decisions
    8. Improved patient satisfaction and experience


--- 2.3 CONCLUSION (1 short paragraph) ---

  Summarise what the literature shows and how it justifies MedBook.
  "The literature reveals that manual health appointment booking systems
  are insufficient to meet modern healthcare demands. Existing digital
  systems in developed countries demonstrate clear benefits, yet none are
  tailored to the Kenyan context. MedBook addresses this gap by providing
  a locally-relevant, affordable, and accessible solution."


================================================================================
CHAPTER THREE – METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

--- KEY DECISIONS TO STATE CLEARLY ---

  Research Design: DESCRIPTIVE (as specified by supervisor)
    Explain: "A descriptive research design was adopted to describe the
    current state of appointment booking systems and the needs of users,
    without manipulating variables."

  Population and Sampling:
    Target group: Patients and administrative staff at a clinic in Nairobi
    Sample size: Aim for 30–50 respondents (realistic for 14 weeks)
    Sampling method: Purposive sampling (explain why – they are the direct users)

  Data Collection:
    Primary: Questionnaire (Appendix 9.1) + optional short interviews
    Secondary: Books, journals, websites (used in Chapter 2)

  Data Analysis:
    Quantitative data from questionnaire → tables and bar charts in Chapter 5
    Use percentages: "60% of respondents indicated that..."

  Testing Plan (for the system):
    Unit testing – each module tested individually
    Integration testing – modules tested together
    User acceptance testing (UAT) – real users test the prototype

  Ethical Considerations:
    - Informed consent obtained from all questionnaire respondents
    - Data collected anonymously – no names on questionnaires
    - Data used only for academic purposes


================================================================================
CHAPTER FOUR – SYSTEMS ANALYSIS AND DESIGN
================================================================================

--- FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS (what the system MUST do) ---

  FR1  – Patients shall be able to register and log in securely
  FR2  – Patients shall be able to search for doctors by specialty and location
  FR3  – Patients shall be able to view doctor availability and book appointments
  FR4  – The system shall send automated SMS/email reminders 24 hours before appointments
  FR5  – Patients shall be able to cancel or reschedule appointments
  FR6  – Doctors/staff shall be able to manage their appointment slots
  FR7  – Admins shall be able to add, edit, and deactivate user accounts
  FR8  – The system shall generate appointment reports for administrators

--- NON-FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS (how the system MUST perform) ---

  NFR1 – Security: Passwords must be hashed (bcrypt). Sessions must time out.
  NFR2 – Usability: System must be navigable by a first-time user within 5 minutes
  NFR3 – Performance: Pages must load within 3 seconds on a standard connection
  NFR4 – Availability: System must be accessible 24/7 (for booking purposes)
  NFR5 – Compatibility: Must work on Chrome, Firefox, and mobile browsers

--- DIAGRAMS – WHAT EACH ONE MUST SHOW ---

  USE CASE DIAGRAM
    Actors: Patient, Doctor, Admin
    Patient use cases: Register, Login, Search Doctor, Book Appointment,
                       Cancel Appointment, View History, Receive Reminder
    Doctor use cases: Login, Manage Availability, View Bookings, Update Status
    Admin use cases: Login, Manage Users, View Reports

  FLOWCHART
    Show the patient booking flow:
    Start → Login/Register → Search Doctor → Select Doctor → Choose Slot →
    Confirm Booking → System sends confirmation → Reminder sent D-1 → Appointment

  CLASS DIAGRAM
    Main classes: User, Patient, Doctor, Admin, Appointment, Specialty,
                  Notification, Report
    Show attributes and methods for each class
    Show relationships (Patient books Appointment, Doctor has Specialty, etc.)

  DATA FLOW DIAGRAM
    Level 0 (Context): One central process "MedBook System", external entities
                       (Patient, Doctor, Admin, SMS Gateway), data stores
    Level 1: Break into sub-processes: User Management, Appointment Management,
             Notification Management, Reporting
    Level 2: Further break down Appointment Management (search, book, cancel,
             update)

  ERD (Entity Relationship Diagram)
    Entities: users, doctors, patients, appointments, specialties,
              notifications, reports
    Key relationships:
      - patient (1) books (many) appointments
      - doctor (1) has (many) appointment slots
      - appointment (1) triggers (1) notification

  SEQUENCE DIAGRAMS
    Admin: Admin logs in → manages users → system updates database → confirms
    User (Patient): Patient searches doctor → selects slot → confirms booking →
                    system saves → sends confirmation → sends reminder

  DATABASE TABLES (document these in 7.4, but plan them here)
    users         (id, name, email, password_hash, role, created_at)
    patients      (id, user_id, dob, phone, address)
    doctors       (id, user_id, specialty_id, bio, location)
    specialties   (id, name)
    appointments  (id, patient_id, doctor_id, slot_datetime, status, notes)
    notifications (id, appointment_id, type, sent_at, status)

CHAPTER FIVE – FINDINGS AND ANALYSIS
================================================================================

--- HOW TO PRESENT DATA ---

  Each finding gets:
    1. A table or bar chart showing the raw responses
    2. 2–3 sentences interpreting what the data means
    3. A link back to the research objective it answers

  Response Rate section:
    "A total of X questionnaires were distributed to patients and administrative
    staff at [clinic name]. Y were returned, representing a response rate of Z%,
    which is considered [adequate/good] for this study."

  Demographics:
    Present: gender split, education level, age range
    Use simple bar charts or pie charts

  SRO 1 findings (challenges):
    Show responses to questions about their experience with the current system
    "X% of respondents agreed that long waiting times were a major challenge..."

  SRO 2 findings (benefits):
    Show responses to questions about desired features of a new system
    "Y% of respondents indicated they would use an online booking system if available..."


================================================================================
CHAPTER SIX – DISCUSSION, CONCLUSION, RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================

  DISCUSSION
    Compare your findings to what the literature said in Chapter 2.
    "The finding that 80% of respondents experienced long waiting times
    corroborates Ndegwa (2019), who noted that Kenyan clinics average a
    2-hour wait time for walk-in patients..."

  CONCLUSION
    Answer the two research questions directly.
    "This study found that the primary challenges of manual systems include
    [list]. The proposed MedBook system addresses these through [features]."

  RECOMMENDATIONS
    For the clinic, for future developers, for policymakers.
    Be specific: "Clinics should invest in reliable internet infrastructure
    to support the adoption of digital booking systems."

  FUTURE WORK
    What you would add if you had more time:
    - Telemedicine / video call integration
    - NHIF insurance verification
    - Mobile application (Android/iOS)
    - Multi-language support (Swahili)

CHAPTER SEVEN – IMPLEMENTATION
================================================================================

  TECHNOLOGIES – be exact, include version numbers when you know them
    Hardware : Laptop/PC running Windows 10/11, min 4GB RAM
    Language : PHP 8.x (Laravel) OR Python 3.x (Django) – decide early, stick to it
    Tools    : VS Code, Git, XAMPP (Apache + MySQL), Postman (for API testing)
    Database : MySQL 8.x
    Frontend : HTML5, CSS3, Bootstrap 5, JavaScript
    SMS API  : Twilio (free tier) or Africa's Talking (Kenyan provider)

  SCREENSHOTS – label every single one
    Format: "Figure X.X – [Description of what is shown]"
    Required screenshots:
      Admin side:  login, dashboard, manage users, view reports
      Doctor side: manage availability, view bookings
      Patient side: register, search doctor, book appointment,
                    confirmation page, appointment history

  USER MANUAL (7.3.2)
    Step-by-step for a patient booking an appointment:
    Step 1: Open browser and navigate to [system URL]
    Step 2: Click "Register" and fill in your details
    Step 3: Log in with your email and password
    ... (continue for every step until appointment confirmed)

ABSTRACT – WRITE THIS LAST
================================================================================

  One paragraph, maximum 1 page (~300 words).
  Follow this exact structure:

  [2 sentences – background and problem]
  [1 sentence – overall objective]
  [2 sentences – methodology: research design, population, data collection]
  [2 sentences – key findings from questionnaire]
  [2 sentences – what the system does / key features]
  [1 sentence – conclusion]
  [1 sentence – recommendation]

APA REFERENCE FORMAT REMINDERS
================================================================================

  Journal article:
    Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. Journal Name,
    Volume(Issue), page–page. https://doi.org/xxxxx

  Website:
    Organisation Name. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Website Name.
    Retrieved from https://...

  Book:
    Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work: Capital letter also for subtitle.
    Publisher.

  Minimum references: 15 (mix of journals, books, and reputable websites)
  All in-text citations must appear in the reference list and vice versa.
