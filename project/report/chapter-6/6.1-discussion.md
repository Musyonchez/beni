# 6.1 Discussion

## 6.1.1 Challenges of Traditional Market Systems (SRO1)

The findings presented in Section 5.3 confirm that the majority of smallholder farmers in the study area sell their produce through intermediaries and express dissatisfaction with the prices they receive. The finding that [X]% of farmer respondents reported selling through a middleman always or most of the time corroborates Kirimi et al. (2011), who documented that Kenyan smallholder farmers in horticultural value chains receive between 20 and 30 percent of the final retail price when transacting through brokers, with the remainder captured by intermediaries at successive stages of the supply chain. The persistence of this dependency, despite the growth of mobile technology in Kenya, suggests that the barrier is not technological awareness but rather the absence of a trusted, accessible platform that farmers can use to reach buyers directly.

The finding that [X]% of farmer respondents experienced significant post-harvest losses in the previous farming season due to the inability to find buyers in time is consistent with estimates from the Food and Agriculture Organization (2018), which places post-harvest losses in Sub-Saharan African horticultural systems at between 30 and 40 percent annually. These losses represent not only a financial cost to individual farmers but a systemic inefficiency that contributes to food insecurity in urban markets, as produce that could feed consumers is instead wasted at the farm level. The research findings thus validate the fourth problem statement identified in Chapter One — that post-harvest losses are a direct consequence of the absence of a fast, direct buyer discovery mechanism.

The reported lack of access to market price information before the point of sale, cited by [X]% of farmer respondents, aligns with the challenge of price opacity documented by Muyanga and Jayne (2014), who found that information asymmetry between farmers and brokers in Kenya consistently disadvantages the former. This finding reinforces the need for a platform that provides farmers with transparent, real-time visibility into demand and pricing, rather than requiring them to negotiate blindly at the point of delivery.

## 6.1.2 Benefits of an Online Agricultural eMarketplace (SRO2)

The findings presented in Section 5.4 demonstrate strong demand-side readiness for a mobile agricultural eMarketplace among both farmers and buyers in the study area. The finding that [X]% of farmer respondents and [X]% of buyer respondents expressed willingness to use such a platform is consistent with the trajectory documented by Aker and Mbiti (2010), who found that mobile phone adoption among agricultural communities in Sub-Saharan Africa correlates positively with willingness to engage in digital commerce when trust and usability barriers are addressed.

The near-universal adoption of M-Pesa among the respondent population — with [X]% of farmers and [X]% of buyers reporting daily or near-daily usage — confirms the findings of Safaricom (2023), which reports over 30 million active M-Pesa users in Kenya. This pre-existing familiarity with mobile money significantly reduces the adoption barrier for FarmLink's integrated M-Pesa payment system, as users would not need to learn a new payment method but simply extend an existing behaviour into a new context.

The independent identification of geolocation-based discovery, M-Pesa payment, order tracking, and seller ratings as the most desired features by respondents — without prompting from the researcher — validates the four core developer features implemented in FarmLink. This convergence between user-expressed needs and the system's designed capabilities suggests that the project has responded effectively to actual market demand rather than assumed requirements. The finding supports the argument made in the literature by Dellarocas (2003) that digital rating and review mechanisms are essential for establishing the buyer trust that offline markets have historically failed to provide for smallholder producers.
