# 1.2 Problem Statement

Despite agriculture being the primary livelihood for the majority of Kenyan households, smallholder farmers continue to face systemic barriers that prevent them from earning a fair income from their produce. The current manual and intermediary-dependent market systems present the following problems:

**Problem 1: Exploitation by middlemen**
The majority of Kenyan smallholder farmers sell their produce through brokers and middlemen who purchase at artificially low prices and resell at a significant profit. Studies indicate that farmers in Kenya receive as little as 20 to 30 percent of the final retail price of their produce, with intermediaries capturing the bulk of the value (Kirimi et al., 2011). This exploitation discourages increased production and perpetuates cycles of rural poverty.

**Problem 2: Absence of a direct digital market channel**
Smallholder farmers currently lack a dedicated digital platform through which they can reach buyers beyond their immediate geographical area. Without such a channel, farmers are confined to selling at local markets where competition is high, prices are low, and market hours are limited. This restricts the volume of produce that can be sold and the income that can be earned.

**Problem 3: Inability to verify produce quality and origin**
Buyers, particularly those in urban areas, are often unable to verify the quality, freshness, or source of produce before purchase. This lack of transparency drives consumer preference toward supermarkets over local farmers, even when local produce may be fresher and more affordable. The absence of a review and rating mechanism further erodes buyer confidence in purchasing directly from farmers.

**Problem 4: Post-harvest losses due to delayed market access**
Kenya loses an estimated 30 to 40 percent of its agricultural produce to post-harvest spoilage annually (Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries, 2019). Perishable goods such as tomatoes, leafy vegetables, and fruits deteriorate rapidly when farmers cannot locate buyers in time. Without a fast and accessible digital channel to connect supply with demand, these losses represent both a food security challenge and a significant financial burden on farming households.
