# 1.1 Background of the Study

The digitalisation of agricultural markets has emerged as a critical strategy for improving farmer incomes, reducing food waste, and strengthening food security across the globe. Online agricultural eMarketplaces leverage mobile technology and internet connectivity to create direct links between producers and consumers, disrupting traditional supply chains dominated by intermediaries. This section examines how similar systems have been implemented in developed countries, in Africa, and within the Kenyan context.

## 1.1.1 Implementation in Developed Countries

**United States of America**
FarmDrop is a community-based online agricultural marketplace founded in 2014 in the United States, designed to connect local farmers and producers directly with consumers through a collaborative storefront model. Rather than requiring each farmer to maintain an independent digital store, FarmDrop aggregates multiple local producers onto a single community storefront, reducing the technical burden on individual farmers while providing buyers with a wide selection of locally sourced produce in one place. The platform operates on a harvest-to-order model in which consumers pre-order from weekly availability listings, allowing farmers to harvest only what has already been sold and thereby significantly reducing food waste. A defining feature of the FarmDrop model is that farmers retain between 85 and 95 percent of the retail sale price — far exceeding the returns available through conventional wholesale and supermarket supply chains (FarmDrop, 2025). This model demonstrates that technology-enabled direct commerce can substantially improve farmer income in high-income markets.

**United Kingdom**
Farmdrop was a UK-based online grocery platform that sourced produce directly from British farms and delivered to consumers, operating between 2012 and 2022. The platform guaranteed farmers up to 75 percent of the retail price — a sharp contrast to the industry norm of 10 to 20 percent offered through conventional supermarket supply chains (Farmdrop, 2021). By offering consumers transparent information about the origin and farming practices behind each product, Farmdrop demonstrated that traceability and direct sourcing are valued by modern consumers. Although the platform ceased operations in 2022 due to unsustainable logistics costs, it established a strong proof of concept for farm-direct digital commerce.

**Australia**
AgriDigital is an Australian agricultural supply chain management and digital marketplace platform that enables grain farmers to sell produce directly to buyers while tracking transactions on a digital ledger. Launched in 2015, the platform has processed over AUD 1.8 billion in commodity transactions and is used by more than 7,000 growers across Australia (AgriDigital, 2023). AgriDigital illustrates how digital platforms can provide smallholder farmers with price transparency, faster payment, and access to a broader buyer network — outcomes that are equally relevant to the Kenyan agricultural context.

## 1.1.2 Implementation in Africa

**Nigeria**
FarmGate Africa is a Nigerian B2B online agricultural commodity marketplace founded in 2018, designed to facilitate large-volume trade between smallholder farmers, farming cooperatives, wholesalers, and retailers. The platform addresses a critical structural problem in Nigerian agriculture — the fragmentation of smallholder produce across thousands of individual small farms — by enabling farmers and cooperatives to aggregate their output and list it in tradeable volumes that are attractive to large-scale commercial buyers. FarmGate Africa integrates with third-party logistics providers to coordinate the physical movement of commodities from rural production areas to urban and industrial demand centres, bridging a gap that has historically been filled by exploitative intermediary networks (FarmGate Africa, 2025). The platform demonstrates that digital aggregation models can unlock commercial markets that would otherwise be inaccessible to individual smallholder farmers operating alone.

**Rwanda**
Rwanda has made significant strides in integrating digital technology into its agricultural sector through the e-Soko platform — a government-backed digital market information system that provides farmers with real-time commodity prices across different markets. The platform enables farmers to make informed decisions about where and when to sell their produce, reducing the information asymmetry that has historically benefited middlemen (Ministry of Agriculture Rwanda, 2018). Rwanda's experience illustrates that even in low-income African contexts, digital market solutions can meaningfully improve farmer welfare when supported by appropriate infrastructure and government policy.

## 1.1.3 Kenya Context

Kenya is one of the most agriculturally active economies in Sub-Saharan Africa, with approximately 75 percent of the population engaged in farming activities, the majority of whom are smallholder farmers cultivating plots of less than two acres (Food and Agriculture Organization, 2020). Despite this agricultural base, farmers consistently earn disproportionately low incomes due to their dependence on brokers and middlemen who control market access. The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics (2022) reports that post-harvest losses across horticultural produce average between 30 and 40 percent annually, representing a significant loss of income and a contributor to food insecurity in urban areas.

Mobile technology penetration in Kenya provides a strong foundation for a digital agricultural eMarketplace. The Communications Authority of Kenya (2023) reports that mobile internet subscriptions exceeded 22 million, representing a penetration rate of over 43 percent. Furthermore, M-Pesa — Safaricom's mobile money platform — has become the dominant method of financial transactions in Kenya, with over 30 million active users transacting billions of shillings monthly (Safaricom, 2023). This infrastructure makes a mobile-first, M-Pesa-integrated eMarketplace both technically viable and accessible to the target population of farmers and buyers. Platforms such as Mkulima Online (2026), a Kenyan digital agricultural listing platform, have demonstrated that local farmers are willing to engage with online marketplaces, yet this platform lacks integrated payment processing, geolocation-based discovery, and formal order tracking — features that are essential for buyer trust and transactional security. FarmLink is designed to build upon this foundation by providing the payment, tracking, and accountability infrastructure that the Kenyan market requires for digital agricultural commerce to scale effectively.
