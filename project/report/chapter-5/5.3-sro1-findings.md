# 5.3 Findings on SRO1: Challenges of Traditional Market Systems

Specific Research Objective One sought to identify the challenges faced by smallholder farmers when using traditional and intermediary-based market systems in Kenya. The following findings are drawn from the farmer respondents' questionnaire responses and are supported by observations gathered during informal interviews conducted at Wakulima Market and in Kiambu County.

## 5.3.1 Dependence on Middlemen

When asked whether they sold their produce through a broker or middleman rather than directly to the final buyer, [X]% of farmer respondents indicated that they did so always or most of the time. Only [X]% reported selling directly to consumers on a regular basis. This finding confirms the structural dependence on intermediaries that has been widely documented in the literature, including by Kirimi et al. (2011), who found that Kenyan smallholder farmers in horticultural chains receive between 20 and 30 percent of the final retail price when selling through brokers.

## 5.3.2 Satisfaction with Prices Received

Farmer respondents were asked to rate their satisfaction with the prices they currently received for their produce on a scale of one to five (1 = very dissatisfied, 5 = very satisfied). The mean satisfaction score was [X] out of 5, and [X]% of respondents indicated that they were dissatisfied or very dissatisfied with the prices they received. During informal interviews, several farmers noted that they felt compelled to accept broker prices because they had no means of comparing prevailing market rates before the point of sale, a challenge consistent with the price opacity documented by Muyanga and Jayne (2014).

## 5.3.3 Post-Harvest Losses

When asked whether they had experienced significant post-harvest losses in the previous farming season due to inability to find buyers in time, [X]% of farmer respondents responded affirmatively. The most commonly cited perishable commodities affected were tomatoes, kale (sukuma wiki), and French beans — all of which are high-turnover horticultural crops grown widely in Kiambu County. This finding aligns with the Food and Agriculture Organization's (2018) estimate that post-harvest losses across horticultural produce in Sub-Saharan Africa average between 30 and 40 percent, and reinforces the urgency of a direct-to-buyer digital channel that reduces the time between harvest and sale.

## 5.3.4 Access to Market Price Information

A further [X]% of farmer respondents indicated that they did not have access to current market prices before deciding where to sell their produce, and [X]% reported travelling to a physical market only to find that prices were lower than expected. This information asymmetry consistently benefits middlemen who have established networks and price knowledge that individual farmers lack. The Financial Sector Deepening Kenya (2019) report notes that limited access to market information is among the primary structural barriers to income growth for Kenyan smallholder farmers — a barrier that a real-time digital platform such as FarmLink is designed to address.

## 5.3.5 Summary of SRO1 Findings

The findings confirm that the four challenges identified in the problem statement — dependence on middlemen, absence of a direct digital channel, inability to verify market prices, and post-harvest losses — are empirically present among the study's farmer respondents. The data demonstrate that the majority of farmers in the study area operate within an intermediary-dominated market structure that consistently limits their income and exposes their produce to preventable losses.
