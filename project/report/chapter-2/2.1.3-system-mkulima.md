# 2.1.3 System Three: Mkulima Online — Kenya

**System Name:** Mkulima Online
**Country:** Kenya
**URL:** https://mkulimaonline.org

Mkulima Online is a Kenyan digital agricultural marketplace established in 2015 and significantly expanded post-2020, designed to connect smallholder farmers directly with buyers including individual consumers and small businesses through a centralised online listing platform. The name "Mkulima" is derived from the Swahili word for farmer, reflecting the platform's farmer-first orientation. Mkulima Online operates as a C2C (consumer-to-consumer) and B2C (business-to-consumer) marketplace in which farmers create profiles and list their produce — including crops, livestock, and agricultural inputs — with photographs, prices, and location details. Buyers browse listings and contact sellers directly via phone or WhatsApp to negotiate terms and arrange delivery or pickup. The platform functions as a digital noticeboard that removes the need for physical middlemen by providing a centralised space where supply and demand can meet without an intermediary capturing value (Mkulima Online, 2026). In addition to its marketplace function, the platform features an educational blog, a virtual agrovet for purchasing agricultural inputs, and a verified seller badge system for trusted farmers. As of May 2026, the platform maintains daily-updated listings across horticultural, livestock, and grain categories, with active farmer presence in regions including Kiambu, Nakuru, and Machakos.

**Strengths:** Mkulima Online's pure marketplace structure is its most significant strength in relation to farmer empowerment. Because transactions are completed directly between farmers and buyers — with payments handled off-platform — farmers retain 100 percent of their sale price, subject only to any applicable listing fee. This gives farmers maximum price control and transparency, in direct contrast to merchant or logistics-led models such as Twiga Foods where the platform determines the farmer's payout. The platform is also highly accessible to low-income farmers, as basic listings are free or low-cost, and the buyer-seller communication occurs through WhatsApp — a tool already widely used across Kenya's farming communities. Mkulima Online is frequently cited in Kenyan agricultural media, including Farmers Trend and Capital FM, as a primary tool for farmers seeking to bypass traditional broker networks.

**Weaknesses:** The absence of integrated payment processing and logistics coordination represents Mkulima Online's primary weakness. Because payments occur directly between buyers and sellers outside the platform, there is no transaction escrow, buyer protection, or fraud prevention mechanism. Buyers must trust the seller based on profile information and listings alone, which can be a significant barrier for high-value trades or first-time interactions between strangers. The platform also lacks geolocation-based produce discovery, a real-time order tracking system, and a formal seller rating and review mechanism — all of which are central features of the FarmLink system. These gaps highlight the opportunity for FarmLink to build upon the Mkulima Online model by adding the trust, transparency, and payment infrastructure that the Kenyan market requires for digital agricultural commerce to scale effectively.
