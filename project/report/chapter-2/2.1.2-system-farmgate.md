# 2.1.2 System Two: FarmGate Africa — Nigeria

**System Name:** FarmGate Africa
**Country:** Nigeria
**URL:** http://www.farmgate.africa

FarmGate Africa is a Nigerian B2B online agricultural commodity marketplace founded in 2018, designed to facilitate large-volume trade between smallholder farmers, farming cooperatives, wholesalers, and retailers. The platform addresses a critical structural problem in Nigerian agriculture — the fragmentation of smallholder produce across thousands of individual small farms — by enabling farmers and cooperatives to aggregate their output and list it in tradeable volumes that are attractive to large-scale commercial buyers. Buyers such as wholesalers, food processors, and industrial retailers can browse commodity listings, negotiate prices directly with sellers, and arrange transactions through the platform. FarmGate Africa integrates with third-party logistics (3PL) providers to coordinate the physical movement of commodities from rural production areas to urban or industrial demand centres, bridging a gap that has historically been filled by exploitative intermediary networks (FarmGate Africa, 2025). As of mid-2025, the platform is recognised as one of the leading agricultural eCommerce startups in Nigeria, operating within a sector that supports the smallholder farmers who produce approximately 90 percent of Nigeria's total agricultural output.

**Strengths:** FarmGate Africa's B2B aggregation model directly addresses the fragmentation problem that prevents individual smallholder farmers from accessing large commercial buyers. By enabling cooperatives to pool produce and list at tradeable volumes, the platform unlocks markets that would otherwise be inaccessible to any individual farmer operating alone. Its integration with third-party logistics providers removes a significant operational burden from the platform itself, allowing it to focus on the marketplace function while leveraging established delivery infrastructure.

**Weaknesses:** FarmGate Africa operates exclusively as a B2B platform, meaning individual consumers cannot purchase directly from farmers through the system. This limits its utility as a tool for empowering farmers to access the premium prices available in direct-to-consumer retail. Additionally, as of mid-2025, the platform remains unfunded according to startup tracking databases, which may constrain its ability to invest in advanced features, expand its logistics network, or compete with better-capitalised platforms in the Nigerian market. The absence of consumer-facing features such as seller ratings, geolocation discovery, and integrated mobile payments represents a significant gap that FarmLink addresses in the Kenyan context.
