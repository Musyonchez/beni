# 2.3 Conclusion

The literature reviewed in this chapter reveals a consistent and well-documented pattern: traditional agricultural market systems in Kenya and across Sub-Saharan Africa systematically disadvantage smallholder farmers through intermediary exploitation, limited market reach, price opacity, post-harvest losses, and the absence of digital financial records. These challenges are not isolated — they compound one another, creating a cycle in which farmers earn too little to invest in their farms, which in turn limits their productivity and deepens their dependence on exploitative market intermediaries.

The analysis of three currently active eMarketplace systems — FarmDrop (USA), FarmGate Africa (Nigeria), and Mkulima Online (Kenya) — demonstrates that digital platforms are being actively adopted across different agricultural contexts and are succeeding in reducing the distance between farmers and buyers. FarmDrop demonstrates that a commission-light model in which farmers retain up to 95 percent of the sale price is commercially viable, though its dependence on physical hub managers limits scalability. FarmGate Africa illustrates how B2B aggregation can unlock larger commercial markets for smallholder cooperatives in an African context, yet its exclusion of individual consumers leaves a significant portion of the market unserved. Mkulima Online is the most contextually relevant system reviewed, operating within the same Kenyan agricultural ecosystem that FarmLink targets and serving a similar user base of smallholder farmers and individual buyers. However, its reliance on off-platform WhatsApp negotiation and cash payments, combined with the absence of geolocation discovery, integrated mobile payments, real-time order tracking, and a formal seller rating system, means it falls short of providing the structured and trustworthy transaction environment that Kenyan farmers and buyers require for digital commerce to scale.

The literature on the benefits of digital agricultural eMarketplaces further confirms that platforms incorporating direct farmer-buyer connection, geolocation matching, mobile payment integration, and peer-review systems produce measurable improvements in farmer income, market efficiency, and consumer satisfaction. These findings collectively justify the development of FarmLink as a locally tailored, consumer-facing, mobile-first eMarketplace that builds upon the foundation established by platforms like Mkulima Online while addressing the critical gaps in payment integration, location-based discovery, order transparency, and seller accountability. The following chapters outline the research methodology adopted to validate these findings within the Kenyan context and present the system design that translates these insights into a functional prototype.
