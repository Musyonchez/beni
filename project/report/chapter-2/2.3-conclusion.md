# 2.3 Conclusion

The literature reviewed in this chapter reveals a consistent and well-documented pattern: traditional agricultural market systems in Kenya and across Sub-Saharan Africa systematically disadvantage smallholder farmers through intermediary exploitation, limited market reach, price opacity, post-harvest losses, and the absence of digital financial records. These challenges are not isolated — they compound one another, creating a cycle in which farmers earn too little to invest in their farms, which in turn limits their productivity and deepens their dependence on exploitative market intermediaries.

The analysis of existing eMarketplace systems — FarmersWeb (USA), Farmdrop (UK), and Twiga Foods (Kenya) — demonstrates that digital platforms have proven effective at improving farmer income and market access in both developed and developing market contexts. However, none of the reviewed systems fully addresses the needs of Kenyan smallholder farmers and individual consumers simultaneously. FarmersWeb and Twiga Foods are restricted to B2B transactions, while Farmdrop — the only consumer-facing platform reviewed — lacked mobile payment integration, geolocation features, and a seller review system, and has since discontinued operations.

The literature on the benefits of digital agricultural eMarketplaces further confirms that platforms incorporating direct farmer-buyer connection, geolocation matching, mobile payment integration, and peer-review systems produce measurable improvements in farmer income, market efficiency, and consumer satisfaction. These findings collectively justify the development of FarmLink as a locally tailored, consumer-facing, mobile-first eMarketplace that addresses the specific gaps identified in both the reviewed systems and the broader literature. The following chapters outline the research methodology adopted to validate these findings within the Kenyan context and present the system design that translates these insights into a functional prototype.
