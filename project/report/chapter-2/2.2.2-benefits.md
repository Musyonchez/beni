# 2.2.2 Benefits of an Online Agricultural Produce eMarketplace

The following section reviews literature related to Specific Research Objective 2: to determine the benefits of adopting an online agricultural produce eMarketplace for Kenyan smallholder farmers.

**1. Direct farmer-to-buyer connection eliminates intermediary exploitation**
The most direct benefit of an online agricultural eMarketplace is the removal of intermediaries from the supply chain, enabling farmers to sell at or close to market price. Aker and Mbiti (2010) demonstrated that mobile-based market information systems in Sub-Saharan Africa reduced price dispersion between farmers and retail markets by enabling direct price negotiation. When farmers can connect and transact directly with buyers, the value that was previously captured by brokers is redistributed between the farmer and the consumer, improving outcomes for both parties.

**2. Expanded market reach beyond local geography**
Digital platforms enable farmers to access buyers across a much wider geographical area than is possible through physical market attendance. Jensen (2007) demonstrated in a landmark study of Indian fishermen that access to mobile-based market information dramatically reduced waste and price volatility by enabling sellers to identify the highest-demand markets before committing to a route. In the context of an eMarketplace, geolocation-based buyer discovery means farmers in peri-urban Nairobi can serve buyers across the entire city, rather than only within walking or cycling distance.

**3. Price transparency and market information access**
An eMarketplace provides farmers with real-time visibility into what other sellers are charging for similar produce, enabling them to price competitively and respond to market conditions. The World Bank (2012) identified price transparency as one of the most significant benefits of digital agricultural platforms, noting that farmers with access to market price data earned consistently higher incomes than those relying on information from brokers. For FarmLink users, the ability to browse competing listings provides both farmers and buyers with the price signals they need to make informed decisions.

**4. Reduction in post-harvest losses through faster buyer discovery**
By enabling farmers to list available produce and connect with buyers immediately, an eMarketplace significantly reduces the time between harvest and sale. Reardon and Timmer (2014) observed that reducing the market discovery time for perishable produce is one of the most impactful interventions for reducing post-harvest losses in developing country agricultural systems. FarmLink's combination of real-time listings and geolocation-based matching is designed specifically to accelerate this connection and reduce the spoilage that occurs when produce sits unsold.

**5. Digital transaction records support financial inclusion**
Every transaction completed through a digital eMarketplace generates a record that can serve as evidence of income and commercial activity. The GSMA (2019) reported that smallholder farmers who transact digitally — particularly through mobile money systems — are significantly more likely to access formal financial services including savings accounts, insurance, and micro-loans. By integrating M-Pesa as the payment mechanism, FarmLink ensures that every sale generates a digital payment record linked to the farmer's mobile number, building a financial history that can unlock broader economic opportunity.

**6. Reduced buyer travel time through geolocation-based matching**
Geolocation features enable buyers to identify farmers and available produce within their immediate vicinity, reducing the time and cost associated with sourcing local food. Tadesse and Bahiigwa (2015) found that proximity-based market matching in digital platforms significantly improved buyer satisfaction and repeat purchase rates in agricultural eCommerce contexts. For urban buyers in Nairobi, the ability to discover a nearby farmer with fresh produce available on the same day represents a compelling alternative to supermarket shopping.

**7. Seller ratings and reviews build trust and incentivise quality**
A peer-review and rating system creates accountability within the marketplace by allowing buyers to publicly evaluate their experience with individual farmers. Dellarocas (2003) established that online reputation systems significantly reduce information asymmetry in eCommerce by enabling buyers to differentiate between high and low quality sellers based on accumulated peer feedback. For FarmLink, a visible farmer rating encourages farmers to maintain produce quality and fulfil orders reliably, since their ability to attract future buyers depends on maintaining a positive reputation on the platform.

**8. Data insights to support better farming and business decisions**
An eMarketplace generates valuable data on buying patterns, peak demand periods, most popular produce categories, and price trends — data that is largely invisible to farmers operating through informal channels. Mittal and Mehar (2012) found that smallholder farmers with access to market data made more informed planting decisions, resulting in better alignment between what they grew and what buyers actually demanded. Over time, FarmLink's transaction data can provide individual farmers with personalised insights into which crops sell fastest, which price points attract the most buyers, and which seasons generate the highest demand — enabling evidence-based farm management.
