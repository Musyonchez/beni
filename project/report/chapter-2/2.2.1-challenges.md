# 2.2.1 Challenges of Traditional and Manual Agricultural Market Systems

The following section reviews literature related to Specific Research Objective 1: to identify the traditional challenges faced by Kenyan smallholder farmers when using manual and intermediary-based market systems.

**1. Exploitation by intermediaries and middlemen**
One of the most extensively documented challenges facing smallholder farmers in Kenya and across Sub-Saharan Africa is the systematic exploitation of farmers by brokers and middlemen who act as gatekeepers to agricultural markets. Kirimi et al. (2011) found that Kenyan smallholder farmers received as little as 20 to 30 percent of the final consumer price for their horticultural produce, with intermediaries capturing the majority of the value added along the supply chain. This price suppression reduces farmer income, discourages investment in farm inputs, and perpetuates cycles of rural poverty.

**2. Limited geographical market reach**
Traditional agricultural selling relies heavily on physical proximity to markets, restricting farmers to selling within their immediate locality. Muyanga and Jayne (2014) observed that Kenyan smallholder farmers are largely confined to selling at local roadside markets or through brokers due to the absence of digital channels that would enable them to reach buyers in urban centres. This geographical limitation results in lower prices due to local supply surpluses and reduces the farmer's ability to identify and respond to demand in higher-value markets.

**3. Price opacity and information asymmetry**
Farmers operating through traditional systems often lack access to real-time market price information, leaving them vulnerable to manipulation by brokers who possess superior market knowledge. The Food and Agriculture Organization (2018) identified information asymmetry as a key structural disadvantage for smallholder farmers in developing markets, noting that farmers who lack access to price data are consistently unable to negotiate from a position of knowledge. This opacity enables middlemen to purchase produce far below the prevailing market price and sell at a significant profit margin.

**4. Post-harvest losses due to delayed market access**
The inability to connect quickly with buyers results in significant post-harvest losses, particularly for perishable produce such as tomatoes, leafy vegetables, and fruits. The Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries (2019) estimated that post-harvest losses in Kenya's horticultural sector average between 30 and 40 percent of total production annually. These losses represent not only lost income for individual farmers but also a broader food security challenge, as significant quantities of nutritious food are wasted before reaching consumers.

**5. High and unpredictable transport costs**
To access markets beyond their immediate locality, farmers must bear the cost of transporting produce to market — often without any guarantee of a sale upon arrival. Okello et al. (2012) documented that transport costs represent a disproportionate share of smallholder farmer operating expenses in Kenya, particularly for those in peri-urban areas where road infrastructure is underdeveloped. Farmers who travel to markets only to find low demand or saturated supply face compounded losses in both transport expenditure and spoiled produce.

**6. Absence of digital transaction records**
Traditional agricultural trade in Kenya is predominantly conducted in cash, with no formal record of transactions maintained by either farmers or buyers. This informality excludes smallholder farmers from access to formal financial services such as loans, insurance, and credit, which typically require documented income and transaction history (Financial Sector Deepening Kenya, 2019). Without a digital financial footprint, farmers are unable to demonstrate creditworthiness to financial institutions and remain locked out of the capital they need to invest in their farms.

**7. High administrative burden on farmers**
Managing sales through traditional channels requires farmers to personally coordinate with multiple buyers, negotiate prices individually, and track payments manually. This administrative workload diverts time and energy away from productive farming activities. Aker (2011) noted that the time cost of market participation — including travel, negotiation, and payment collection — represents a significant hidden cost borne by smallholder farmers in sub-Saharan Africa that reduces the net benefit of market engagement.

**8. Seasonal income instability and absence of forward orders**
Traditional market systems do not provide mechanisms for advance or forward ordering, meaning farmers have no visibility into future demand when making planting decisions. This forces farmers to make production decisions based on historical patterns rather than actual buyer demand, resulting in supply-demand mismatches and volatile seasonal income (World Bank, 2020). During harvest peaks, local market saturation depresses prices; during off-seasons, farmers have no orders to fulfil. This instability makes financial planning and household investment extremely difficult for smallholder farming families.
