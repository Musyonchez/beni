# 2.1.2 System Two: Farmdrop — United Kingdom

**System Name:** Farmdrop
**Country:** United Kingdom
**URL:** https://www.farmdrop.com (service discontinued 2022)

Farmdrop was a UK-based online farm-to-consumer grocery platform that operated from 2012 to 2022. The platform connected individual consumers with local British farms, enabling buyers to order fresh produce online for home delivery. Farmdrop positioned itself as an ethical alternative to supermarkets by guaranteeing farmers a significantly higher share of the retail price — up to 75 percent in some cases — compared to the industry average of 10 to 20 percent offered through conventional supermarket supply chains (Farmdrop, 2021). The platform featured a curated product catalogue, transparent sourcing information, consumer-facing farmer profiles, and a subscription delivery model. At its peak, Farmdrop partnered with over 200 farms across the United Kingdom and served tens of thousands of consumers in London and surrounding areas.

**Strengths:** Farmdrop's consumer-facing model directly addressed the disconnect between urban buyers and local farmers, creating a transparent and trust-based marketplace. The platform's commitment to fair farmer pricing — paying up to 75 percent of retail — stands in stark contrast to the exploitative pricing common in traditional supply chains. The inclusion of detailed farmer profiles and produce sourcing information gave consumers confidence in the quality and origin of their food, a feature that is directly relevant to the design of FarmLink's seller rating and profile system.

**Weaknesses:** Farmdrop's primary weakness was its dependence on an in-house logistics and cold-chain delivery infrastructure, which proved financially unsustainable and ultimately led to the platform's closure in January 2022 (The Guardian, 2022). The platform was also limited to an urban consumer base in the United Kingdom and did not incorporate mobile payment integration, making it inaccessible to users in markets where mobile money dominates. This case offers an important design lesson for FarmLink: logistics and delivery coordination should remain outside the platform's scope in its initial version to avoid the cost overruns that led to Farmdrop's failure.
