# 2.1.1 System One: FarmDrop — United States of America

**System Name:** FarmDrop
**Country:** United States of America
**URL:** https://farmdrop.us

FarmDrop is a community-based online agricultural marketplace founded in 2014 in the United States, designed to connect local farmers and producers directly with consumers through a collaborative storefront model. Unlike traditional eCommerce platforms where each farmer maintains an independent store, FarmDrop aggregates multiple local producers onto a single community storefront, reducing the individual technical burden on farmers while providing buyers with a diverse selection of locally sourced produce in one place. The platform operates on a harvest-to-order model in which consumers pre-order from weekly availability listings, allowing farmers to harvest only what has already been sold and thereby significantly reducing food waste. A key operational feature of FarmDrop is the designated Market Hub Manager — a community coordinator responsible for receiving bulk harvest deliveries and distributing individual customer orders for pickup. This role removes the need for individual last-mile delivery by each farmer, making the logistics model sustainable and community-driven (FarmDrop, 2025).

**Strengths:** FarmDrop's most notable strength is its farmer-first revenue model. Farmers retain between 85 and 95 percent of the retail sale price, with the remainder covering platform fees and the hub manager's coordination fee — a distribution that far exceeds what farmers typically earn through wholesale or supermarket supply chains. The platform also eliminates the need for individual farmers to build or manage their own websites by providing shared digital infrastructure, lowering the barrier to entry for smallholder producers. The harvest-to-order approach further reduces post-harvest waste by ensuring produce is only harvested once a confirmed sale exists.

**Weaknesses:** FarmDrop's model depends heavily on the presence of an organised and committed Market Hub Manager in each community. In areas without a motivated community lead, the model cannot function, which limits its scalability in rural or dispersed agricultural regions. The platform also does not incorporate mobile payment integration, geolocation-based farmer discovery, or a seller rating and review system — features that are essential for the mobile-first, trust-building context of the Kenyan market that FarmLink targets.
